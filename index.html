ISTM 476 · A research method you can actually run

How a company case study gets built, one phase at a time.

This is the path from a blank page to a sourced financial argument: scope the question, mine the assigned reading, find the filings, pull the numbers, chart them, and synthesize. The two middle steps are live tools. Everything traces to a real source, so the work is legible, not magic.

The pipeline

Inputs

The assigned chapter, the assignment rubric, and (for a team) the agreed scope. Read all three before writing anything.

0

Scope the deliverable in scope

Decide exactly what the section has to answer. Here: two to four lessons other firms can copy. Out of that falls a skeleton of claims, no numbers yet.

1

Mine the primary text in scope

Pull the raw claim for each lesson straight from the chapter. This is the spine that matches the assignment, and most of the time it is enough on its own.

!

The fork: stop, or verify with numbers?

After mining the text you choose how deep to go. Both are real research. Only one matches a book-discussion assignment.

Stay in scopeWrite the chapter-only version. Short, safe, on-assignment.
Go deeper (out of scope)Verify every claim against the filings and chart it. Rigorous, and easy to over-build past what was asked.
2

Find the reports deeper

Locate the company's SEC filings: the 10-K (annual), 10-Q (quarterly), 8-K (events). The Report Finder does this live against EDGAR.

Open the Report Finder
3

Dissect the filing deeper

Pull the line items into a time series and compute the instruments: operating margin, capex intensity, the cash conversion cycle. The Dissector does this on bundled companies or any filing you drop in.

Open the Filing Dissector
4

Chart and synthesize deeper

Turn the verified series into exhibits, then find the one idea underneath them. For Amazon that idea is that every moat is also the liability bolted to it.

5

Produce and source always

Lay it out, and keep the sources in tiers: from the chapter, verified in the filings, or flagged as outside estimate. A reader should always know which is which.

The case behind the tools

These tools were factored out of one assignment: the lessons section of an Amazon case (Gallaugher, Chapter 8). The chapter argues four transferable lessons, and the deep version verified each against Amazon's SEC filings. That verification is what the Report Finder and Filing Dissector let you reproduce for yourself.

1
Cost centre becomes the product
2
The product isn't the product
3
Patience as a moat
4
The flywheel is moat and rot

Try it: open the Dissector, load Amazon, and watch the cash conversion cycle sit near minus fifty days. That negative number is lesson three made concrete: Amazon is paid before it pays, so growth funds itself.
